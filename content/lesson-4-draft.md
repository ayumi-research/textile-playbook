# Lesson 4: When Cheap Becomes Expensive

**Quick Summary:**  
That €3 cap quote looks like a win until you're explaining to your client why their logo is peeling off after three washes. The real cost isn't the price tag — it's what happens after.

**Why This Saves You:**  
Learn to spot the five cheap traps before you fall in. Save the relationship, not just the budget line.

---

## The Five Cheap Traps

### Trap 1: The Peeling Logo (Durability vs. Price)

**The pitch:** "Same design, €3 vs €7. Big savings on 500 pieces!"

**What they didn't mention:**
- €3 version: DTF transfer on cheap cotton, heat-pressed at lower temp to save time
- €7 version: Embroidery on quality fabric, proper stitch density, quality backing

**The math:**
```
Cheap option: 500 × €3 = €1,500
Replacement after complaints: 500 × €3 = €1,500
Client relationship: damaged, possibly lost
Total cost: €3,000 + reputation

Quality option: 500 × €7 = €3,500
Replacement needed: 0
Client happy, reorders: 2,000 pieces next quarter
Total cost: €3,500, future revenue secured
```

**The rule:** Cheap becomes expensive when the item is meant to last. For one-time event giveaways, cheap is fine. For workwear, client gifts, or anything worn more than 5 times, cheap is a trap.

---

### Trap 2: The Rush Fee Surprise (Timeline vs. Price)

**The pitch:** "We can do it in 3 days, no extra charge."

**What they didn't mention:**
- They're skipping their own quality check step
- They're outsourcing to a cheaper/faster shop with no accountability
- If it's wrong, there's no time to fix it

**The scenario:**
Conference is Friday. You order Monday with Tuesday delivery promised. Tuesday: nothing. Wednesday: "Minor delay, quality issue, Thursday for sure." Thursday: arrives at 6pm, half the order is wrong sizes, logos crooked. You stay up until 2am fixing what you can. Conference Friday: you're exhausted, half the team has no shirts, you look disorganized.

**The real cost:** Your sleep, your stress level, your professional credibility.

**The rule:** Rush quotes that seem too good usually are. Realistic timelines have buffer. "No extra charge" rush often means "we're cutting corners you can't see."

---

### Trap 3: The Hidden Setup (Quote vs. Total)

**The pitch:** "€4.50 per piece, cheapest around!"

**What they didn't mention:**
- €80 digitizing fee (not included)
- €25 per color change (your 3-color logo = €50)
- €60 rush fee (their "standard" timeline is 4 weeks, you need 2)
- €120 shipping ("not included in quote")
- €40 sample fee (mandatory for first orders)

**The math:**
```
Sticker price: 200 × €4.50 = €900
Actual invoice: €900 + €80 + €50 + €60 + €120 + €40 = €1,250
"Cheapest around" reality: €6.25/piece, not €4.50

Competitor's "expensive" quote: €5.80/piece, all-in, delivered
Actual savings by going "cheap": negative €90
```

**The rule:** Always ask for "all-in delivered price including digitizing, samples, shipping, and timeline." Then compare. Sticker price is fiction.

---

### Trap 4: The Single-Point-of-Failure (Supplier Reliability)

**The pitch:** "We're a small shop, personal service, great prices!"

**What they didn't mention:**
- One embroiderer, no backup
- If they're sick, your order stops
- If their machine breaks, no contingency
- If they're overwhelmed, you're waiting

**The scenario:**
Small shop, great prices, lovely owner. You order 300 pieces for a major client event. Week of production: owner's mother has emergency surgery. Shop closes for a week. Your order is half-done, sitting in a box, no one else can finish it because the files are in their proprietary format.

**The real cost:** Missed event, embarrassed in front of client, emergency sourcing at 3× price from a shop that can actually deliver.

**The rule:** Cheap small shops are fine for small, non-critical orders. For mission-critical, deadline-sensitive, client-visible work, you need operational redundancy. That costs more. It's insurance.

---

### Trap 5: The Correction Cascade (Error Compounding)

**The pitch:** "We'll fix it, no problem!"

**What they didn't mention:**
- "Fixing it" takes 2 weeks
- "Fixing it" costs €200 shipping back and forth
- "Fixing it" means the client gets half their order on time, half late
- "Fixing it" once means you're now watching them like a hawk, spending hours on coordination

**The scenario:**
Order arrives, color is wrong (supplier's "navy" is your "royal blue"). They agree to redo. You ship back. They redo. New color is closer but still not right. Third attempt, finally correct. Three weeks late, you've spent 10 hours on emails and calls, client is annoyed, you've paid triple shipping.

**The real cost:** Time, stress, client patience, your energy for other work.

**The rule:** One mistake is fixable. A pattern of mistakes is a supplier who doesn't have their process sorted. Cheap often correlates with "we'll fix it" culture instead of "we'll get it right" culture.

## When Cheap Is Actually Fine

**Cheap works when:**
- One-time event (trade show giveaway, conference bag stuffers)
- Short lifespan expected (cotton bags for grocery shopping)
- Non-critical branding (internal team shirts, not client-facing)
- Ample time buffer (order arrives, you inspect, you have 2 weeks to fix if needed)
- You have supplier history (you know their "cheap" is still "reliable")

**The test:** "If this goes wrong, what's my exposure?" Low exposure = cheap okay. High exposure = cheap dangerous.

---

## The True Cost Formula

**Price × Risk Factor = True Cost**

| Factor | Multiplier | Applies When |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Client-facing | ×2 | Client logo, client event, client gift |
| Deadline-critical | ×1.5 | Event date, launch date, no buffer |
| Quality-sensitive | ×1.5 | Workwear, long-term use, premium positioning |
| Single supplier | ×1.3 | No backup option, sole source |

**Example:**
- €3 cap, internal team, flexible timeline, backup supplier available: True cost = €3 × 1.0 = €3 (cheap is fine)
- €3 cap, client gift, conference deadline, no backup: True cost = €3 × (2 × 1.5 × 1.3) = €11.70 (cheap is actually expensive)

## The "One-Third Rule"

From field experience: **The cheapest third of quotes is usually hiding something.**

**The three tiers:**
- **Bottom third:** Cutting corners you haven't discovered yet
- **Middle third:** Fair price, fair value, reasonable expectation
- **Top third:** Premium pricing, maybe premium value, maybe just premium ego

**Practical approach:** Start with middle third. Build relationships. Eventually you learn which bottom-third suppliers are actually efficient (rare) and which top-third suppliers are worth it (occasional).

## Real Example: The €2,000 "Savings"

**The order:** 1,000 polo shirts for company rebrand, client-facing, deadline critical.

**Supplier A (cheap):** €6.50/piece, "all-inclusive," 2-week turnaround
**Supplier B (mid):** €8.20/piece, includes samples, 3-week turnaround

**The choice:** A saves €1,700. Go with A.

**What happened:**
- Week 1: No sample provided despite request. "We'll make it right in production."
- Week 2: Delivery day, shirts arrive. Logos are 2cm lower than proof, colors slightly off.
- Week 3: Emergency re-order from Supplier B at rush prices (€12/piece), 500 pieces only, delivered day before event.
- Original 1,000 shirts: donated to charity, unusable for client-facing event.

**The math:**
```
"Savings": €1,700
Actual cost: (500 × €12) + (1,000 × €6.50) = €6,000 + €6,500 = €12,500
What B would have cost: 1,000 × €8.20 = €8,200
True "savings" from going cheap: -€4,300
```

**The lesson:** When the answer to "can I see a sample first?" is anything other than "yes, here's when," the cheap price is a trap.

---

## How to Ask About True Cost

**Don't:** "What's your cheapest price for 500 polos?"

**Do:** "I'm ordering 500 polos for a client event. Walk me through what could go wrong and how you prevent it."

**What you learn:**
- Supplier A: "Nothing ever goes wrong, we're the best!" → Alarm bells
- Supplier B: "Polo sizing varies by brand, we always send a size sample. Color matching, we do a thread sample first. Logos, we position on a shirt like yours before full run." → Process thinking

Process thinking costs more upfront. It saves you downstream.

---

## Red Flags: Cheap Becoming Expensive

**The supplier who...**
- Can't explain *why* they're cheaper (efficiency vs. cutting corners)
- Rushes past your questions to get the deposit
- Has no samples, no references, no recent work photos
- Quotes significantly below market without clear reason
- Says "trust me" instead of showing you

**Your response:** Walk away. Cheap isn't the problem. Unexplained cheap is.
