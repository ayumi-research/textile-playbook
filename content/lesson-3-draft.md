# Lesson 3: Three Questions That Expose Process Gaps

**Quick Summary:**  
Most suppliers aren't scammers — they're disorganized. These three questions expose where their process breaks down before you're holding a box of wrong-size polos with crooked logos.

**Why This Saves You:**  
Catches the "we'll figure it out" approach before it becomes your problem.

---

## The Real Risk Categories

From thousands of orders, problems cluster here:

| Risk Category | What Actually Goes Wrong | Cost |
|---------------|-------------------------|------|
| **Existence** | Ghost company, deposits disappear, nothing ships | 100% loss + deadline blown |
| **Product** | Wrong sizes, poor quality fabric, color mismatch vs. sample | Reweigh or unusable stock |
| **Decoration** | Logo size/shifted, wrong colors, messy backside on embroidery | Unusable, client rejection |
| **Delivery** | Late, rushed packing, no quality check, logos not cleaned | Missed event + bad first impression |
| **Service** | Unreachable when problems arise, no prototyping, rigid process | No path to fix errors |

**Good news:** Most reputable suppliers have solved the first two (existence/product). The danger zone is **decoration + delivery + service** — where promises meet reality.

---

## The Three Questions

### Question 1: "Walk me through your logo approval process — what do I see before production starts?"

**What this reveals:**

| Answer Quality | What It Means |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Strong:** "You get a digital proof within 24h, then a physical sample on actual fabric for orders over €500, then final production approval" | Multi-stage review, physical verification, clear checkpoints |
| **Medium:** "We send a PDF proof, you approve, we produce" | Standard, but no fabric sample, color relies on screen calibration |
| **Weak:** "Just send us the file, we'll match it" or "We don't do proofs" | No process, hope-based production, prepare for surprises |

**The gap this exposes:**
Suppliers who skip physical samples assume your screen colors match their thread colors. They don't. Navy on your monitor becomes purple on their machine, and you're stuck with 200 purple polos.

**What you actually need:**
- Digital proof (size, position, layout)
- Physical sample on target fabric (color, texture, quality)
- Explicit approval checkpoint before full production run

---

### Question 2: "What does your quality check step look like — and when does it happen?"

**What this reveals:**

| Answer Quality | What It Means |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Strong:** "Every piece checked before leaving, loose threads trimmed, backside support removed if embroidery, steamed/pressed, individually bagged" | Pride in output, proper finishing, protective packaging |
| **Medium:** "We check a few from each batch" | Spot-checking, some problems slip through |
| **Weak:** "The embroiderer checks their own work" or "We trust our process" | No independent QC, no finishing step, you receive production-line output |

**The gap this exposes:**
"We trust our process" means "we don't have one." You receive items straight from the machine — loose threads, hoop marks, backing still attached, folded hastily into boxes. Your client opens the box and their first impression is amateur hour.

**Red flags to watch for:**
- "We don't have time for individual checks on large orders" (quality scales down with quantity)
- "It's the embroiderer's responsibility" (no oversight)
- "Quality is built into the process" (means no explicit QC step)

**What you actually need:**
Explicit post-production handling: inspection, finishing (trimming, pressing), protective packaging. Not assumed — described.

---

### Question 3: "If I need a last-minute change or have a concern mid-production, how do I reach you and how fast do you respond?"

**What this reveals:**

| Answer Quality | What It Means |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Strong:** "We have a team of [X] people covering support — you reach us by phone/Signal/WhatsApp, typical response under 2 hours during business hours. If urgent, we escalate to production manager directly." | Clear channel, defined response time, escalation path exists |
| **Medium:** "Email us, we respond within 24 hours" | Async only, no urgency handling, shared inbox syndrome |
| **Weak:** "Leave a message, someone will get back to you" or vague hand-waving about "the team" | No ownership, message black hole, panic when problems arise |

**Note on team-based support:**  
Large-volume shops (e.g., StickX, major wholesalers) often rotate support across a team rather than assigning dedicated account managers — especially for smaller orders. This isn't necessarily bad. The key is whether **someone specific is reachable quickly** and whether **urgent issues get escalated** to someone with authority. "We have a team of 4, you'll get whoever's available, 2-hour response typical, production manager handles escalations" is actually a solid answer.

**The gap this exposes:**
When your client changes the logo the day before production, or the sample arrives and the color is wrong, you need someone who answers *now*. Suppliers with no named contact or slow channels leave you watching deadlines evaporate while "someone" eventually checks their email.

**The "prototyping service" angle:**
Ask if they offer pre-production samples on demand. Good suppliers: "Yes, €X for a single piece, takes Y days." Bad suppliers: "We prefer to go straight to production" or "That'll cost the same as the full run."

**What you actually need:**
- Named human responsible for your order (not "the team")
- Responsive channel (phone/Signal/ WhatsApp, not just email)
- Pre-production sample option with clear cost/timeline

## How to Score Responses

**Scoring rubric:**

| Question | 2 points | 1 point | 0 points |
|----------|----------|---------|----------|
| **Approval process** | Physical sample + digital proof + explicit approval | Digital proof only | No proof or "send file and trust us" |
| **Quality check** | Every piece, finishing included, described specifically | Spot check or "built into process" | No check or "embroiderer checks own work" |
| **Responsiveness** | Named contact, fast channel, prototyping available | Email-only, 24h response, unclear ownership | No named contact, vague responsiveness, no samples |

**Total score:**
- **6 points:** Solid operational foundation
- **4-5 points:** Manageable with explicit written agreements
- **0-3 points:** High risk of "we'll figure it out" becoming "you figure it out"

## Real Example: The Unreachable Rush

**Situation:** Client needs 100 polos for Friday event. Orders Monday with Tuesday delivery promised.

**Monday PM:** "Everything on track."
**Tuesday AM:** No delivery. Email sent. No response until 4pm: "Minor delay, Wednesday AM."
**Wednesday AM:** Nothing. Phone goes to voicemail. Email unreturned. Client has no polos for Friday setup.
**Thursday:** Supplier finally responds — "Embroidery machine broke, sourcing from partner." Partner delivers Thursday PM. No quality check. Polos arrive wrinkled, some with backing still attached, sizes mixed up.

**The questions that would've caught this:**
1. "Approval process?" → If no physical sample on rush timeline, color/shift risk is high
2. "Quality check?" → If no finishing step, expect raw production output
3. "Contact and response time?" → If no named human + fast channel, expect communication black holes

**Result:** Client wears competitor's generic shirts. Supplier blamed "machine issues." Real cause: no process for rush accountability.

---

## When to Ask These Questions

**Before deposit.** Always.

**Best format:** Phone or video, not email. Watch for hesitation or "let me check with the team" — that team doesn't exist or isn't accessible.

**Follow-up:** Get commitments in writing (email recap). Vague promises become specific terms once money changes hands — or they don't, and you have proof of what was promised.

---

## The Meta-Pattern

Every supplier *wants* to deliver good work. The difference is **system vs. heroic effort**:

- **System:** Same result every time because process handles it
- **Heroic effort:** Same result *this time* because someone cared enough to fix problems on the fly

Heroic effort scales badly. When volume hits or staff changes, quality drops. Systems scale. The three questions expose whether they're running a system or hoping for heroes.

---

## Pro Tip: The Prototype Investment

**Request a prototype before committing to full production.**

- **Cost:** Free for small items (some suppliers) or €20-50 for complex pieces
- **Time:** Adds 1-2 weeks to total timeline
- **Payoff:** Eliminates 80% of pre-service risk

**When to prototype:**
- First order with a new supplier
- New technique or fabric you haven't used before
- Complex logo placement (chest + sleeve + back)
- Color-critical work (brand colors must match)
- Rush orders where you can't afford a redo

**The math:** A €30 prototype that catches a color shift is cheaper than a €2,000 re-run of wrong-color polos. For long-term supplier relationships, prototypes are cheap insurance against expensive assumptions.
